Fitch Ratings has published a 2017 Outlook for South American Banks (excluding Brazil), highlighting mixed degrees of robustness against global market conditions.
South American banks have generally demonstrated solid loan quality ratios since the commodities price shock of 2014, despite sluggish economic growth, currency volatility and narrower margins. Fitch expects loan quality indicators to be pressured mildly as loans season through the less favourable growth cycle. In Venezuela and Argentina, inflation-induced credit growth should continue to support near-term loan quality ratios.
Sector outlooks diverge, however, with respect to performance and capitalization. Peruvian and Chilean banks stand out for their resilient financial metrics, a trend Fitch expects to continue in 2017. Colombian banks, however, will continue to adapt to tighter interest margins and pressured capital stemming from a sharp local currency depreciation and changes in accounting standards.
With the exception of Ecuador and Venezuela, bank regulation is progressing toward alignment with Basel III capital rules, albeit with local particularities and at different paces. These rules are phasing in higher quality capital requirements, including loss absorbing secondary capital and international standards for liquidity coverage.
With the exception of Venezuela (where Fitch sees no near term stabilization in operating conditions), Fitch expects banks in the region to continue holding up well against global headwinds. Banks have largely adjusted to currency and commodity price volatility and major electoral and climate downside risks failed to materialize. As a result, sector outlooks could eventually improve in line with recovering credit growth, improved financial performance, and in Colombia, recovered solvency indicators.
