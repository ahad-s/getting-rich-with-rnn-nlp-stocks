NEW YORK--(BUSINESS WIRE)--
Fitch Ratings has downgraded approximately $2 billion of the state of Kansas department of transportation (KDOT) highway revenue bonds to 'AA-' from 'AA+'.
The Rating Outlook is Stable.
SECURITY
The bonds are special obligations of the state of Kansas secured by a gross lien on and payable from revenues received in the State Highway Fund (SHF) and transferred to the Highway Bond Debt Service Fund.
KEY RATING DRIVERS
The downgrade reflects the application of Fitch's revised U.S. Tax-Supported Rating Criteria, published on April 18, 2016. Underlying credit factors since the time of Fitch's last review of KDOT are stable; however, under the new criteria, the structure does not meet the requirement for rating a dedicated tax bond without a consideration of the operating risks of the state of Kansas. Although the strength of the pledged revenues supporting the highway revenue bonds would warrant a higher rating, the credit quality of the bonds is limited by the exposure of the SHF to general state operations.
GROWTH PROSPECTS STEADY: Most of the transportation-related revenues pledged to the bonds, including constitutionally-dedicated motor fuels taxes and motor vehicle receipts, have been fairly stable over time, with occasional volatility resulting from shifts in pledged federal transportation aid. On average, the revenue trend supporting the bonds has been positive given the addition of pledged state sales tax revenue that has benefitted from increased allocations via legislative action.
ESTABLISHED PROGRAM AFFECTED BY STATE ACTIONS: KDOT's bond program is a well-established part of a comprehensive and legislatively authorized long-term transportation infrastructure program. Management strengths include active revenue monitoring, multi-year forecasting, and the ability to adjust capital spending in response to repeated state actions to siphon off KDOT funds and direct them toward general state operations.
REVENUE STREAM RESILIENCY: Given limited expected leveraging of the pledged revenues and current high debt service coverage, the structure can absorb a decline in revenues expected to result from a moderate recession scenario.
STRONG LEGAL PROVISIONS: A 3.0x maximum annual debt service (MADS) test for additional bonds limits leverage of pledged resources. Management practices have sustained coverage well in excess of this requirement.
LINKAGE WITH STATE OPERATIONS: KDOT's operations are not distinct from general state operations. Interaction with the state's operations, including sizable transfers to the General Fund and KDOT support for unrelated state programs, has led to periodic fund shifts and cancellation of KDOT capital projects, which Fitch expects will continue in the future.
RATING SENSITIVITIES
LINK TO STATE OPERATING RISKS: The rating is sensitive to our view of the operating risks of the state of Kansas.
MAINTENANCE OF AMPLE COVERAGE: The rating is also sensitive to the continued maintenance of ample coverage by pledged revenues.
CREDIT PROFILE
The downgrade of KDOT's highway revenue bonds to 'AA-' from 'AA+', reflects the application of Fitch's Tax-Supported Rating Criteria, published in April 2016. The program's strengths continue to include a solid 3x ABT and comprehensive management oversight. However, given frequent shifts in KDOT funds to general state operations based on general fund budgetary needs and the state's repeated alterations to the components and/or distribution formulas of the revenues credited to the fund, Fitch views KDOT's bonds as not being sufficiently insulated from the state's general operations.
Operating risks associated with the state include financial operations that have featured an ongoing structural imbalance in recent years. The state has implemented recurring measures to address the imbalance, but has also relied on one-time resources, including transfers from the SHF and pension payment deferrals. A persistent trend of revenues underperforming projections has contributed to the fiscal stress, and led to the need for successive mid-year budget adjustments.
ESTABLISHED PROGRAM; STEADY REVENUE GROWTH PROSPECTS
The program is supported by a diverse stream of broad-based revenues in the SHF and a strong 3x MADS additional bonds test. Bond repayment receives a gross pledge of all revenues in the SHF, including motor fuel taxes, a portion of the state's general sales and use tax, and federal transportation aid. A subset of pledged SHF revenues are constitutionally dedicated for transportation and include 66.37% of state motor fuel tax receipts and a percentage of vehicle registration and license fees. Combined, these constitutionally dedicated revenues alone totaled roughly $515 million in fiscal 2016 (unaudited, cash basis), providing healthy 2.8x coverage of debt service.
Sales and use taxes have grown to represent approximately 37% of SHF revenues; $519 million in fiscal 2016, up from 26% in fiscal 2013, as the SHF's allocation statutorily increased effective July 1, 2013. Effective July 1, 2015, the state increased the sales tax rate to 6.5% from 6.15% as part of its fiscal 2016 gap-closing measures but the SHF's share of the revenue matched that expected from the July 1, 2013 revision. To accomplish that target, the SHF's share of sales tax receipts was changed to 16.226% of receipts in fiscal 2016 and to 16.154% in fiscal 2017. The higher share in fiscal 2016 incorporates a one-month lag in revenue collections.
Federal aid accounted for a sizable 23% of SHF revenue in fiscal 2016. KDOT has forecast modestly increasing support from federal aid in future fiscal years corresponding with current federal transportation aid authorization while continuing to target annual debt service coverage of 4x from state-source revenues alone.
SHF revenues for fiscal 2016, less federal funds, increased 1.2% from fiscal 2015, covering debt service by a solid 5.3x; including federal funds, total revenues decreased by 2.2% as federal revenue fell by 12% from the prior year due to a decline in federal project reimbursements. Coverage in fiscal 2016 from revenues as defined by the bond resolution, which includes federal aid but excludes local revenue, with Fitch's exclusion of a Build America Bond subsidy, is currently estimated at 7.3x. MADS coverage from these same revenues is estimated at 6.9x while constitutionally-dedicated revenue alone cover MADS by 2.5x.
SHF revenues for fiscal 2017 are expected to increase by 3.5% largely due to a boost in federal aid. Absent the increase in federal aid, most state-derived revenue sources are expected to modestly decrease, incorporating KDOT's conservative forecast for these revenues. Overall, KDOT forecasts relatively flat growth in pledged revenues over the next several years, which Fitch views as reasonable.
MULTI-YEAR PLANNING HAMPERED BY STATE DRAWS
KDOT's mission is to plan, develop, and operate various modes of transportation across the state. KDOT's current transportation improvement program, Transportation Works for Kansas (T-WORKS), runs through fiscal 2020 and is expected to fund $7.6 billion in projects over the life of the program. Debt issuance is dependent upon the availability of SHF revenues rather than a statutory dollar limitation, and about $1.175 billion in total bonding is expected to be issued over the course of the program; KDOT reports no current plans for additional borrowing. The balance of the capital program is funded from cash on-hand in excess of debt service requirements.
Over the past several years, KDOT's pay-go capital program has been impacted by state mandates to send increasing funds to assist with statewide budget shortfalls. Funds unrelated to the transportation program have been transferred from KDOT to the state's General Fund, the department of aging and disability services for mental health grants, and to the department of administration for debt service related to statehouse renovations. Transfers from KDOT to other operating funds have grown from $119 million in fiscal 2013 to $542 million in fiscal 2017. In response to statewide revenue shortfalls when developing the fiscal 2017 budget, KDOT delayed $525 million in capital projects in fiscal years 2017 through fiscal 2019. KDOT recently announced a further delay of $32 million in capital projects in fiscal 2017 in response to the state's budget uncertainty.
HIGH RESILIENCY IN DOWNTURN SCENARIO
To evaluate the sensitivity of the dedicated revenue stream to cyclical decline, Fitch considers the results of the Fitch Analytical Sensitivity Tool (FAST), using a 1% decline in national GDP scenario, as well as assessing the largest decline in revenues over the period covered by the revenue sensitivity analysis. Based on a 15-year pledged revenue history, FAST generates a 5.8% scenario decline in pledged revenues. Pledged revenues could withstand a 67% decline, assuming full leveraging to the ABT, or almost 11.6x the scenario output, a high level of resiliency.
Outstanding bonded debt totals just over $2 billion, 16% of which is outstanding in a variable-rate mode. KDOT has floating to fixed rate swaps in place for these bonds.
Additional state guidance on leveraging includes permitting issuance of highway revenue bonds so long as MADS on all outstanding and proposed bonds does not exceed 18% of SHF revenues for the current or any future fiscal year; this 18% cap does not alter the 3.0x additional bonds test. The cap was suspended for fiscal years 2016 and 2017 to accommodate two factors: an early draw of federal funds in fiscal 2015 that reduced SHF revenues in fiscal 2016 and greater than expected debt service expense in fiscal 2017 from a larger bond issuance.
