China’s exports gained, snapping a seven-month losing streak in dollar terms, as a cheaper yuan aided foreign purchasing. Imports jumped the most in two years.
Overseas shipments increased 0.1 percent from a year earlier in November, compared with estimates in a Bloomberg survey projecting a 5 percent decline. Imports rose 6.7 percent, versus a projected 1.9 percent decrease, leaving a  $44.6 billion trade surplus.
The currency has fallen 10 percent against the dollar since a surprise devaluation in August 2015 while remaining stable recently against a basket of currencies. Exports stabilizing suggests demand remains intact for now as the world’s largest exporter faces potential headwinds and policy uncertainty as Donald Trump prepares to take office Jan. 20.

"Better-than-expected trade data out of China today reflects both an uptick in global demand as well as the continued strength of the domestic economy," said Julian Evans-Pritchard, an economist at Capital Economics in Singapore. "Despite today’s positive surprise, the medium-term outlook for Chinese trade remains challenging."
Exports to the U.S., China’s largest trading partner, snapped a streak of losses, rising 6.9 percent. Shipments to the European Union also turned positive, climbing 5.1 percent.
